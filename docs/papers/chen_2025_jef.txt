ABSTRACT
Subrahmanyam (1991) presents a model in which increased variance in liquidity trades reduces
price efficiency when market makers are risk-averse. Motivated by this theoretical insight, we
hypothesize that pent-up demand from lottery-seeking investors amplifies their overreactions
to news, leading to larger short-term return reversals. Consistent with this hypothesis, we
identify a significant pattern in weekly U.S. stock returns for lottery-like stocks, defined by
high recent maximum daily returns (MAX). Specifically, high-MAX stocks that were past 1week losers (or winners) exhibit notably positive (or negative) returns in the following week.
Applying a short-term reversal strategy to high-MAX stocks generates an average weekly return
of 1.66%, significantly outperforming the 0.65% return from the same strategy applied to lowMAX stocks. This result remains robust even after controlling for market microstructure biases
and survives a series of robustness tests. Interestingly, the MAX-enhanced reversal strategy
proves effective only when retail order imbalance is in the highest quintile. This result holds
across both value-weighted and equal-weighted portfolios, underscoring the pivotal role of retail
investors. Taken together, our findings highlight a new channel through which retail investorsвАЩ
preference for lottery-like payoffs amplifies their overreactions, enhancing the profitability of
short-term reversal strategies.

1. Introduction
Subrahmanyam (1991) presents a model demonstrating that increased variance in liquidity trades reduces price efficiency,
assuming either market makers or privately informed traders are risk averse.1 Motivated by these theoretical insights, we hypothesize
that lottery-seeking investorsвАЩ trading activity has a pronounced impact on stock prices, amplifying volatility. In response, riskaverse market makers may temporarily scale back liquidity provision, leading to lower price efficiency and higher anomaly profits.
Furthermore, investorsвАЩ preference for lottery-like payoffs may intensify their overreaction to news, leading to a predictable pattern
of price inefficiency that manifests in short-term reversals.
Consistent with this hypothesis, we discover a novel empirical pattern among stocks with lottery-like characteristics. We
document that lottery-like stocks can experience significantly larger positive or negative weekly returns, depending on whether they

significant profits.
Our study also builds on Lehmann (1990) and Cooper (1999), who show that short-term reversal strategies generate substantial
profits in weekly U.S. stock returns, attributing this to investor overreaction to news. However, these studies do not identify the
underlying cause of this overreaction. We address this gap by linking investor overreaction to their preference for lottery-like payoffs,
proxied by the maximum daily return (MAX) variable introduced by Bali et al. (2011).
Our main contributions to the related literature are multifaceted. First, since our primary interest is in the return patterns in
weekly stock returns, we extend the work of Bali et al. (2011) by showing that the MAX anomaly also exists in weekly U.S. stock
returns. Specifically, we find that high-MAX stocks from the prior week significantly underperform low-MAX stocks for at least two
subsequent weeks. This suggests that the MAX anomaly is more pervasive than previously thought, occurring in both monthly and
weekly stock returns.
Second, aligning with our main hypothesis that investorsвАЩ demand for lottery-like characteristics in stocks can exacerbate their
overreaction to news, we find that, when stocks are double-sorted on both MAX and past 1-week returns, high-MAX stocks that were
also past 1-week losers (winners) earn significantly positive (negative) average returns, resulting in a highly profitable short-term
reversal strategy. Strikingly, this reversal strategy implemented on high-MAX stocks significantly outperforms the same strategy
implemented on low-MAX stocks. In our view, this stark contrast bolsters our premise that lottery-demand induced overreaction
significantly contributes to the success of short-term reversal strategies.
More specifically, we feature an important temporal separation between the timing of MAX and past returns in our main analysis.
We first sort stocks into quintile portfolios based on their MAX in week рЭС° вИТ 2 in order to identify stocks with lottery-like features.
Within each MAX quintile, we then further sort stocks based on their returns in week рЭС° вИТ 1, which we use as a proxy to capture
positive or negative news flows for each stock during that week. Intriguingly, we report that high-MAX loser (winner) stocks earn
an average weekly return of 0.89% (вИТ0.77%) in an equal-weighted portfolio in week рЭС°, resulting in a weekly average return of
1.66% for the weekly reversal strategy in our sample from July 1963 to December 2022. In comparison, a similar reversal strategy
implemented on low-MAX stocks only yields an average weekly return of 0.65%, with past 1-week losers (winners) earning 0.43%
(вИТ0.22%). Thus, the MAX effect widens the return differential between past short-term losers and past short-term winners, consistent
with our hypothesis that investorsвАЩ obsession with lottery like payoffs exacerbates their overreaction and leads to higher reversal
profits.
To ensure the robustness of our baseline results regarding the measurement of a stockвАЩs lottery characteristics, we first explore
alternative measures of MAX. We report that our main results remain consistent when estimating MAX using different time frames:
4-week, 13-week, and 26-week windows. Additionally, we create a new lottery likeness index variable, calculated by averaging the
z-scores of maximum daily return, idiosyncratic skewness, and idiosyncratic volatility. Our findings remain remarkably consistent
when we apply these alternative measures to assess a stockвАЩs lottery characteristics.
Further bolstering our main hypothesis, in a triple-sorted portfolio analysis, we examine the interaction between retail order
imbalance, MAX, and past weekly returns. Our findings indicate that the MAX-enhanced reversal strategy generates significant
returns only when retail order imbalance is in the highest quintile. This pattern holds across both value-weighted and equal-weighted
portfolios, supporting the view that retail investors drive overreaction by chasing lottery-like stocks.
Our main findings withstand a comprehensive series of robustness tests. They are evident in both portfolio analysis and FamaвАУ
MacBeth cross-sectional regressions. They remain consistent across risk adjustments based on the Fama and French (1992, 2015)
factors, along with an additional momentum factor, as well as the Q-factor model proposed by Hou et al. (2015). Notably, our
results are highly significant across two distinct sub-periods, which is particularly impressive considering that the unconditional
short-term reversal strategy has weakened significantly in recent years due to increased market liquidity and the decimalization of
price quotes.
To further address concerns about potential biases induced by market microstructure, we apply methodologies from Blume and
Stambaugh (1983) and Asparouhova et al. (2010), confirming that our findings remain robust even after controlling for these biases.
Additionally, we demonstrate that the MAX-enhanced short-term reversal strategy achieves comparable performance when portfolios
are constructed using residual returns from the FamaвАУFrench three-factor model, as shown in Blitz et al. (2013).
Moreover, our findings on the divergence in portfolio performance between high-MAX past short-term winners and losers
challenge existing theories on investorsвАЩ lottery-like preferences. Barberis and Huang (2008) propose a model where investors, in
addition to holding the tangency portfolio, include a security with positively skewed payoffs in equilibrium. Bali et al. (2011) argue
that the MAX effect supports this model, as the skewed securities in their framework generate negative average returns, consistent
with high-MAX stocks. However, our results suggest that this model falls short in explaining the dynamic patterns we observe,
particularly the significantly positive returns earned by high-MAX past short-term losers.
An et al. (2020) find that the performance of lottery-like stocks depends on reference points, with stocks having high capital
gains overhang (CGO) generally outperforming those with low CGO. However, since a stockвАЩs classification as a 1-week winner or
loser is likely unrelated to its CGO вАФ measured over a five-year horizon вАФ our results remain puzzling within the framework of their
reference-dependent preference theory. Furthermore, our findings cannot be solely explained by fluctuations in investor sentiment
(Baker and Wurgler, 2006), as the MAX-enhanced reversal strategy is effective regardless of whether investor sentiment is high or
low.
In summary, this study advances the literature by offering new empirical insights into investorsвАЩ preferences for lottery-like
stocks, short-term reversals, and their interaction. We provide strong evidence that short-term reversals are more pronounced

2. Discussion of related literature and hypothesis development
The ideas in this paper are related to two distinct branches of the existing literature: short-term reversals in stock returns and
investorsвАЩ preference for lottery-like payoff. We discuss the related literature on these two important topics as well as the main
hypothesis for this study.
2.1. Stocks with lottery-like payoffs
There exists ample evidence that investors often chase highly skewed returns that resemble lotteries. For example, Bali, Cakici,
and Whitelaw (2011) investigate investorsвАЩ preference for lottery-like stocks and find a significant negative cross-sectional relation
between expected stock returns and maximum daily returns in the past month. Eraker and Ready (2015), Byun and Kim (2016),
Green and Hwang (2012), and An, Wang, Wang, and Yu (2020) study investorsвАЩ lottery-like preference in the context of OTC stocks,
options, IPO stocks, and anomalies, respectively. In an interesting study, Liu et al. (2020) show that the demand for lottery-like
stocks is stronger ahead of earnings announcements. In contrast to the standard underperformance of these stocks, lottery-like
stocks outperform non-lottery stocks by about 52 basis points in the 5-day window ahead of earnings announcements. A partial list
of other related studies includes: Boyer et al. (2010), Mitton and Vorkink (2007), Conrad et al. (2013) and Conrad et al. (2014).
Kumar (2009) provides strong evidence that confirms the close connection between retail investorsвАЩ demand for stocks with
lottery features and their propensity to gamble. He documents that the same social-economic factors that induce greater expenditure
in lotteries are also linked to greater investments in stocks with lottery-like features. One of the key implications of KumarвАЩs study is
that state lotteries and lottery-type stocks can act as complements. Gao and Lin (2015) and Dorn et al. (2015) find direct evidence
of this substitution effect between state lotteries and lottery-like stocks with data from Taiwan and Germany, respectively.
The model developed by Barberis and Huang (2008) offers a theoretical explanation of investors demand for stocks with lotterylike characteristics under the framework of Tversky and KahnemanвАЩs (1992) cumulative prospect theory. With the help from a
novel equilibrium concept with nonunique global optima, they find that contrary to the prediction of the standard expected utility
theory, a stockвАЩs idiosyncratic skewness is priced. In other words, a positively skewed stock can be вАШвАШoverpricedвАЩвАЩ and can earn a
negative average excess return in their model. Bordalo et al. (2013) construct an alternative theory to Barberis and Huang (2008),
and argue that payoff salience drives investorsвАЩ demand for risky assets. We note that both models are only capable of explaining
the unconditionally negative average returns earned by lottery-like stocks, but they are silent on why returns on lottery-like stocks
are dependent upon their recent loser/winner status.
An, Wang, Wang, and Yu (2020) report that the performance of lottery-like stocks are reference-dependent, where the reference
prices are based on the CGO variable proposed by Grinblatt and Han (2005). After double-sorting stocks on MAX and CGO, An
et al. (2020) find that in their sample, high-MAX stocks severely underperform low-MAX stocks only when CGO is low. When
CGO is high, high-MAX stocks actually outperform low-MAX stock by 0.54% per month. They conjecture that this result is likely
driven by investorsвАЩ reference-dependent preference. Specifically, low-CGO stocks are where investors have capital losses. Due to the
break-even effect and the aversion to loss realization, investors with capital losses often take the chance that can potentially recover
their prior losses, and the urge to break even can induce investors with prior losses to take risky gambles that they otherwise would
not have taken. We point out that reference-dependent preference hypothesis proposed by An et al. (2020) is unlikely to explain
our main results for the following two reasons. First, they find that lottery-like stocks earn negative returns when there is a capital
loss, but we find that it is the high-MAX past winner stocks that underperform. At least on a short-term basis, owners of past winner
stocks should carry a capital gain rather than loss. Second, a stockвАЩs status as 1-week winner or loser is likely unrelated to its CGO,
which is often computed with a long-term (5-year) rolling window.
2.2. Short-term reversals
Contrary to the tenets of efficient market hypothesis, empirical studies have consistently demonstrated the potential for active
trading and investing strategies to achieve significant excess returns based on a stockвАЩs past return patterns. These strategies can be
broadly categorized into two types: momentum strategies, which involve buying past short-term winners and selling past short-term
losers, and reversal strategies, which involve buying past short-term losers and selling past short-term winners. While momentum
strategies tend to perform well over the medium-term, typically three to twelve months, contrarian strategies have shown success in
both the very short-term (within a month, e.g. Jegadeesh (1990) and Lehmann (1990)) and the very long-term (three to five years).
The existing literature posits that short-term contrarian profits are most likely driven by non-informational liquidity shocks
(e.g. Grossman and Miller (1988), Campbell et al. (1993), Avramov et al. (2006), and Nagel (2012)). We point out that this
explanation seems plausible and is consistent with the fact that unconditionally profits to short-term reversal trading strategies

However, we also believe that liquidity provision is likely to be only one of many contributing factors that can account for the
success of short-term reversal strategies. The most likely alternative explanation seems to be investorsвАЩ overreaction to news, and
should be accredited to Lehmann (1990), who argued forcefully that вАШвАШthe predictability of equity returns may reflect the overreaction

A number of studies have investigated the interaction between short-term reversal and other important economic or market
variables. For instance, Da et al. (2014) study the impact of fundamental news, and find that an enhanced reversal strategy based on
residual returns after controlling for cash flow news are much more profitable than the standard short-term reversal strategy. Hameed
and Mian (2015) propose an intra-industry reversal strategy and find that reversal strategies based on industry-adjusted returns are
more likely to capture deviations from fundamental values. Cheng et al. (2017) find that fluctuations in liquidity provisions triggered
by the exit of institutional investors can significantly impact short-term reversals.
2.3. Main hypothesis and empirical implications
In this study, we aim to deepen our understanding of the psychological foundations behind investorsвАЩ overreaction and its
effects on short-term market reversals. We hypothesize that investorsвАЩ attraction to lottery-like payoffs may be a key driver of this
overreaction. To test this hypothesis, we propose investigating the following empirical implications.
First, if investorsвАЩ preference for lottery-like stocks contributes to their overreaction to news, then short-term reversal trading
strategies should be more effective when applied to stocks with lottery-like characteristics compared to those without. To see this,
we point out that investors with lottery preferences act as uninformed liquidity traders in the sense of Kyle (1985) model. Thus, as
shown by Subrahmanyam (1991), under the assumption that either privately informed traders or market makers are risk-averse, an
increase in the variance of liquidity trades (due to lottery preference in the current context) should reduce price efficiency and lead
to higher reversal profits √† la Campbell et al. (1993).
Second, we anticipate that the MAX-enhanced short-term reversal strategies will be more profitable for smaller firms and less
liquid stocks. This is due to the fact that the pent-up demand from lottery-seeking investors is likely to exert a greater influence
on the prices of small and illiquid stocks, thereby amplifying their volatility. Following the reasoning of Subrahmanyam (1991), an
increase in the variance of these stocks should deter risk-averse market makers, resulting in higher returns for reversal strategies in
these scenarios.
Third, we further conjecture that retail investors are likely to dominate the population of investors who chase lottery-like stocks.
There is ample empirical evidence that suggest retail investors are unsophisticated and are more susceptible to various behavioral
biases such as overconfidence (e.g., Barber and Odean (2000). As such, we propose to test this empirical implication by exploring
the relation between retail trading activities and short-term reversal strategies implemented on stocks with lottery-like features.
3. Data description
Our main sample consists of domestic common stocks (share code 10 or 11) listed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE),
American Stock Exchange (AMEX), and National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations (NASDAQ) from July 1963
to December 2022. We rely on both the monthly and daily data files from the Center for Research in Security Prices (CRSP) database
to obtain stock price, trading volume, shares outstanding, exchange codes, and so on. The CRSP daily data files are used to help
construct weekly stock returns. Financial statement data are collected from the Compustat database. The FamaвАУFrench factors are
from Ken FrenchвАЩs website. The q-factor model data are from Lu ZhangвАЩs website. In addition, we also use the investor sentiment
data provided by Jeffrey Wurgler.
Since our empirical work mainly focuses on weekly returns, we construct the (weekly) maximum daily return variable (MAX)
following the ideas from Bali, Cakici, and Whitelaw (2011). Specifically, MAX is defined as the largest daily return in a week. As
noted by Bali, Cakici, and Whitelaw (2011), the MAX variable is a simple and intuitive measure of investorsвАЩ demand for lottery-like
payoffs. We verify that the weekly MAX behaves similarly to Bali et al.вАЩs (2011) monthly MAX variable. To ensure robustness, we
also confirm our main findings in monthly returns.
To explore the implications from the reference-dependent preferences hypothesis of An et al. (2020), we run tests based on the
capital-gains-overhang (CGO) variable from Grinblatt and Han (2005). Specifically, we compute the CGO measure for each stock,
based on prices and turnover over the past 5 years. The reference price for a given stock as follows:

4. Main empirical results
4.1. The MAX anomaly in weekly stock returns
Since our primary interest is in reversals in weekly stock returns, we first present evidence about the existence of Bali et al.вАЩs
(2011) MAX anomaly in the weekly data. Table 1 of Bali et al. (2011) report that, from 1962 to 2005, the high minus low valueweighted (equal-weighted) decile portfolios sorted on MAX from the past one month has an average monthly return of вИТ1.03%
(вИТ0.65%). In other words, unconditionally lottery-like stocks are known to severely underperform non-lottery-like stocks in monthly
data.
As far as we know, we are the first in the related literature to report that the MAX anomaly also exists in weekly U.S. stock
returns. In Panel A of our Table 1, we form quintile portfolios by sorting stocks on the MAX variable from the past week at рЭС° вИТ 1.
We report both equal-weighted and valued-weighted average weekly portfolio returns from week рЭС°. Consistent with evidence from
monthly data, we find that the equal-weighted high minus low MAX portfolio earns an average weekly excess return of вИТ0.43%
(t-statistic = вИТ11.71). Likewise, the value-weighted portfolio has an average weekly excess return of вИТ0.35% (t-statistic = вИТ7.96).
Similar results are obtained after risk-adjustments by all three factor models.
In Panel B, we form quintile portfolios by sorting stocks on the MAX from week рЭС° вИТ 2. We skip week рЭС° вИТ 1 and report the average
weekly portfolio returns from week рЭС°. In this case, we find that the equal-weighted high minus low MAX portfolio has an average
weekly excess return of вИТ0.11% (t-statistic = вИТ3.27). Similarly, the value-weighted portfolio has an average weekly excess return
of вИТ0.11% (t-statistic = вИТ2.65).
Taken together, the results from Table 1 indicate that there is strong evidence that the MAX anomaly is highly significant in
weekly stock returns and the effect seems to persist for at least two weeks after portfolio formation.

stocks significantly outperform the same strategy implemented on low-MAX stocks.

4.4. Results based on the alternative measure of investorsвАЩ lottery preference
Bali, Cakici, and Whitelaw (2011) establish that the MAX variable is a robust indicator of investorsвАЩ lottery preferences. However,
other measures, such as idiosyncratic skewness and idiosyncratic volatility, are also commonly used in the literature. To ensure that
our main results are not specific to the MAX measure, we introduce a Lottery Likeness Index (LLI) in Table 4, which is constructed
by averaging the z-scores of MAX, idiosyncratic skewness, and idiosyncratic volatility. Idiosyncratic skewness and idiosyncratic
volatility are estimated using the methodology from Kumar (2009). The estimation period for all three variables (MAX, idiosyncratic
skewness, and idiosyncratic volatility) span from week рЭС° вИТ 2 to week рЭС° вИТ 27. The LLI is designed to capture the common variations
among these three variables.
In Table 4, we form double-sorted portfolios (5 by 5) every week from July 1963 to December 2022 by sequentially sorting stocks
on the LLI and lagged weekly stock returns. We report that our main findings remain intact after replacing MAX with the LLI. For
instance, the equal-weighed (value-weighted) high-LLI reversal portfolio earns an average excess weekly return of 1.83% (1.28%),
whereas the low-LLI reversal portfolio can only generate an average weekly excess return of 0.35% (0.43%). Similar patterns are
observed when adjusting for various benchmark asset-pricing models. Notably, average returns decrease monotonically from high-LLI
to low-LLI stocks, underscoring the strong relation between reversal portfolio returns and investorsвАЩ lottery preferences.

Our baseline results from Section 4 support our main hypothesis: that investor overreaction, driven by a preference for lotterylike payoffs, can lead to profitable short-term reversal strategies in weekly returns. In this section, we aim to further test additional
implications that could strengthen the overreaction hypothesis.

First, we anticipate that the price impact of investor overreaction will be more pronounced for stocks with lottery-like
characteristics, especially among smaller and less liquid firms. Second, according to the overreaction hypothesis, retail investors

5.1. Evidence from amihud illiquidity measure
At least since Campbell, Grossman, and Wang (1993), the literature has recognized that liquidity provision plays an important
role in contributing to short-term reversals in stock returns. Is it true that our main results are mainly driven by the liquidity effect?
To fully address this question, in Table 5, we formally test this alternative hypothesis. We introduce triple sorts (3 by 5 by 5)
formed each week from July 1963 to December 2022 by sequentially sorting stocks based on the Amihud illiquidity measure (at
week рЭС° вИТ 1), maximum daily return (MAX, at week рЭС° вИТ 2), and weekly stock returns (PRET, at week рЭС° вИТ 1). Panel A presents the results
for equal-weighted portfolios, while Panel B reports the results for value-weighted portfolios. Within each panel, we further analyze
the results based on different levels of illiquidity: high, middle, and low.
Consistent with previous research such as Campbell, Grossman, and Wang (1993) and Nagel (2012), our findings demonstrate
that stocks with higher levels of illiquidity (or equivalently lower liquidity) exhibit more pronounced short-term reversals due to
their inherent high volatility. Moreover, Table 5 reveals that highly illiquid stocks with high MAX values that were also previous
winners display the lowest returns in the following week for both equal-weighted and value-weighted portfolios, and vice versa for
high-MAX past loser stocks.
Importantly, we report that the short-term reversal strategy works well regardless of stock liquidity. For example, the mean
differences in short-term reversal portfolio returns across high-MAX vs low-MAX stocks are highly significant even among stocks
with low illiquidity (high liquidity). This conclusion holds for both equal-weighted and value-weighted portfolios. We also notice
that when comparing the average returns of short-term reversal portfolios side by side across the two panels, the mean returns of
various illiquidity portfolios are remarkably consistent regardless of the benchmark models used. Taken together, we conclude that
while liquidity provision does seem to contribute to the overall profitability of short-term reversal strategies, it cannot explain the
significant differences in average returns between high-MAX and low-MAX short-term reversal portfolios. Therefore we conclude
that our main findings cannot be explained by the liquidity effect.
Some readers might be concerned that due to illiquidity, it is possible that our findings could be artificially inflated by market
microstructural biases. Later, in Table 11, we provide additional robustness checks to alleviate this concern.
5.2. Evidence from share turnover
To verify that the results in Table 5 are not specifically related to the Amihud illiquidity measure, we replicate the analysis in
Table 6 using share turnover instead. Share turnover is an alternative, widely-used proxy for stock liquidity, and this comparison
helps ensure that our findings are not an artifact of the Amihud illiquidity measure.
The results in Table 6 align closely with those in Table 5, reinforcing our findings. Specifically, weekly reversal strategies applied
to high-MAX stocks consistently outperform those applied to low-MAX stocks, regardless of turnover levels. For instance, when
turnover is high, the equal-weighted high-MAX (low-MAX) reversal portfolio generates a highly significant average weekly excess
return of 1.56% (1.07%). The differential between high-MAX and low-MAX returns is 0.49% per week, with a рЭС°-statistic of 6.79.
Similarly, when turnover is low, the high-MAX minus low-MAX return differential remains highly significant at 1.27% per week.
Results from value-weighted portfolios exhibit the same pattern. Thus, while share turnover may partially account for variations in
average returns across reversal portfolios, it does not explain the performance differences between high-MAX and low-MAX reversal
portfolios.
We also perform a similar analysis using a third measure of stock liquidity вАФ the bidвАУask spread. Details of this analysis are
provided in the appendix (Table A1). Our findings remain consistent across different liquidity measures, reinforcing the robustness
of our results.

5.4. Evidence from retail trading
It is reasonable to hypothesize that investors who pursue stocks with lottery-like payoffs are likely retail investors. This hypothesis
stems from the fact that individual investors, unlike institutional investors, often lack sophistication and are more prone to
psychological biases. For instance, Barber and Odean (2001) have shown that retail investors from a large U.S. discount brokerage
exhibit behavioral biases such as overconfidence. These investors tend to chase stocks that capture attention, resulting in significant
underperformance compared to the market due to excessive trading.
Grinblatt and Keloharju (2009) further analyze data from Finland and find that Finnish retail investors who engage in frequent
trading are motivated by overconfidence and a desire for excitement. Similarly, Barber et al. (2022) observe that retail clients of
the online broker Robinhood are influenced by attention-driven trading behaviors.
In Table 8, we present a triple-sorted portfolio analysis based on retail order imbalance, MAX, and past weekly returns. We adopt
the methodology of Boehmer et al. (2021) to track retail orders. Their approach classifies a trade as initiated by retail investors if it is
an off-exchange market order with a subpenny component in the execution price. The retail imbalance is calculated by aggregating
both buy and sell order imbalances using data from the TAQ database, covering the period from January 2010 to December 2019.
Our findings reveal that the MAX-enhanced reversal strategy is effective only when retail order imbalance is in the highest quintile.
Specifically, the high-MAX minus low-MAX reversal strategy yields statistically significant average returns exclusively in high retail
imbalance scenarios. In contrast, average returns are not significant in other conditions, a pattern observed consistently across both
value-weighted and equal-weighted portfolios. We conclude the overall evidence from retail trading activities in Table 8 is consistent
with view that retail investors tend to chase stocks with lottery-like characteristics, aligning with the overreaction hypothesis.
6. Robustness tests
In this section, we explore several alternative hypotheses and conduct a series of additional robustness tests. Specifically, we
examine alternative hypotheses related to investorsвАЩ reference-dependent preferences and investor sentiment. Additionally, we
assess the robustness of our findings by addressing market microstructure noise and present results based on residual returns and
FamaвАУMacBeth (1973) cross-sectional regressions.
6.1. Reference dependent preferences
Grinblatt and Han (2005) show that a stocksвАЩ capital-gains-overhang (CGO), a turnover-weighted average of past prices,
can predict medium-run momentum behavior. An et al. (2020) provide interesting evidence that lottery-like stocks significantly
underperform non-lottery-like stocks when investors have lost money as indicated by a low CGO, and vice versa when investors
have large gains in the stock. For example, in their Table 2, An et al. (2020) report that among the lowest quintile CGO stocks,
the high-MAX minus low-MAX return spread is вИТ1.38% (рЭС°-statistic = вИТ5.35) per month. In contrast, among the highest quintile CGO
stocks, the high-MAX minus low-MAX return spread is 0.54% (рЭС°-statistic = 2.30) per month. Therefore, An et al. (2020) concludes
that anomalies associated with lottery-like stocks are reference-dependent вАФ investors are driven by the desire to break-even on
stocks they have paper losses. In fact, their aversion to loss realization is so strong that investors would double down on their losses
and hope that their gamble will pay off.

6.2. Investor sentiment
Stambaugh et al. (2012) find that investor sentiment plays a key role in explaining a wide range of anomalies in cross-sectional
stock returns. In this subsection, we propose to study the relation between investor sentiment and the MAX-enhance short-term
reversal anomaly.
Table 10 displays the returns of reversal portfolios based on double sorting (5 by 5) of the maximum daily return (MAX, at week
рЭС° вИТ 2) and the past weekly return (PRET, at week рЭС° вИТ 1), with the portfolios conditioned on investor sentiment levels. The investor
sentiment index, derived from Baker and Wurgler (2006), is orthogonal to key macroeconomic variables. The results are presented
for three levels of the investor sentiment index based on its value at week рЭС° вИТ 1.
The evidence in Table 10 suggests that our main findings are not driven by shifts in investor sentiment. Notably, short-term
reversal strategies perform better under both high and low sentiment conditions. For example, among value-weighted portfolios,
when investor sentiment is high, the high-MAX (low-MAX) reversal portfolio yields an excess weekly return of 1.54% (0.56%).
In contrast, when investor sentiment is low, the high-MAX (low-MAX) reversal portfolio achieves an excess weekly return of
1.21% (0.55). In both scenarios, these strategies outperform those observed when sentiment is at a moderate level. This U-shaped
relationship challenges the alternative hypothesis that investor sentiment is the main driver behind the effectiveness of the MAXrelated reversal strategy, as the strategy remains robust even during periods of low sentiment. Similar patterns are observed with
equal-weighted portfolios. Overall, the results from Table 10 indicate that while the MAX-enhanced reversal effects are influenced
by investor sentiment levels, the relationship is not monotonic but rather exhibits a U-shaped pattern.
6.3. Accounting for market-microstructure noises
Since our main results are more prominent among small-cap and illiquid firms, some readers might wonder how market
microstructure noises induced by large bidвАУask spread might have affected our results. To address this issue, we rely on the
methodologies proposed by Blume and Stambaugh (1983) and Asparouhova et al. (2010). More specifically, these authors
demonstrate that microstructure noises can introduce an upward bias in estimated average returns.
To offset the effect from microstructure noises, Asparouhova, Bessembinder, and Kalcheva (2010) propose a methodology that
computes portfolio returns weighted by the prior period observed gross (one-plus) returns. In Table 11, we present results following
this methodological adjustment. We find that our baseline results from Table 2 remain valid after these methodological adjustments.
For example, short-term reversals among high-MAX stocks are much more profitable than the same strategy implemented on lowMAX stocks (1.68% vs 0.65%). These results are intact after risk adjustments. In addition, the spreads between high-MAX and
low-MAX stocks are quite large for both past short-term losers and past short-term winners. For example, the high-MAX past shortterm losers can earn a 0.85% average weekly return with a significant рЭС°-statistic of 10.56, which outstrips their low-MAX counterparts
by a margin of 0.44% per week. Interestingly, we also find that the average weekly returns decrease (increase) monotonically for
past short-term losers (winners) across the five MAX quintile portfolios. Therefore, we conclude that our main findings reported
earlier are reaffirmed after controlling for market-microstructure noises.

7. Conclusion
In this article, we examine the interplay between investorsвАЩ preferences for lottery-like stocks and short-term reversals in stock
returns, utilizing the MAX variable from Bali et al. (2011) as a proxy for investorsвАЩ preference for lottery-like payoffs. We present
several significant findings.
First, we confirm that the MAX anomaly identified by Bali et al. (2011) also manifests in weekly U.S. stock returns. Notably, the
low average returns experienced by high-MAX stocks persist for at least two weeks.
Second, while existing literature indicates that unconditionally lottery-like stocks yield negative returns, we provide strong
evidence that, depending on their classification as past short-term winners or losers, these stocks can achieve either positive or
negative average returns. Specifically, we demonstrate that high-MAX past 1-week losers can earn outsized positive mean returns,
while high-MAX past short-term winners may experience negative mean returns compared to their low-MAX counterparts. This
finding is robust across various conditions; it is particularly pronounced among small and illiquid firms, yet remains valid even
after controlling for market microstructure biases.
Third, by analyzing the difference in mean returns between high-MAX past short-term losers and winners, we find that a
short-term reversal strategy can generate significantly higher expected returns among high-MAX stocks. Furthermore, the average
returns of this strategy decline consistently as we move from high-MAX to low-MAX stocks. This establishes that investorsвАЩ demand
for lottery-like stocks significantly influences the performance of short-term reversal strategies. Importantly, our findings remain
consistent even after a comprehensive series of robustness checks, including controls for reference-dependent preferences, investor
sentiment, and seasonality. We also observe that MAX-enhanced reversal portfolios perform well among stocks with high retail order
imbalance.
We believe that our findings align with and complement the theoretical insights of Subrahmanyam (1991) , who posits that when
market makers are risk-averse, an increase in the volatility of uninformed liquidity trades can lead to reduced price efficiency.
Consistent with this framework, our results indicate that the pent-up demand from lottery-seeking investors can intensify their
overreaction to news about lottery-like stocks, prompting market makers to become more cautious, which in turn results in higher
abnormal reversal profits.
